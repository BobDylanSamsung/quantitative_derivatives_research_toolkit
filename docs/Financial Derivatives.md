A derivative is a contract between a seller and buyer who's value is determined by the fluctuations in value of an underlying asset or group of assets.

There are two types of derivatives:
- Locks bind both parties to the terms of the agreed contract
- Options give the right but not the obligation to the holder to buy or sell the underlying asset

The most common derivative types are:
- Options
- Futures
- Swaps
- Forwards

Examples of the underlying assets include:
- shares, indices, commodities, interest rates, currencies and bonds. 

[[Options Trading|Options]]
Contracts that give you the right (not requirement) to take a specific action in the future such as purchasing a stock if it will benefit you. 

**Futures**
A contract between two parties to trade an asset at an agreed upon price at a future date. Futures can be used to hedge risk or speculate on the price of an asset. The buyer and seller are obligated to fulfil the contract. Futures trade on an exchange.

**Forwards**
Similar to futures but trade only over the counter and not on an exchange. As an OTC product, forwards carry greater counterparty risk

**Swaps**
A contract where parties exchange cash flows or liabilities of different financial instruments. The most common swap is an interest rate swap, often between an fixed and variable interest rate. The purpose of a swap is to manage risk, obtain funding at a better rate or to speculate on future differences between the cash flows.
