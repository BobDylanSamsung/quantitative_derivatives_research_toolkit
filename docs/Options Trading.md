Options are traded through brokerages that act as intermediaries between buyers and sellers. The terms of these transactions are locked at the time of the contract.

Options traders will typically do so for one of two reasons: 
- To protect their other investments (hedging)
- To profit from an assets price moves without purchasing the asset itself (speculating)
# Key Terms
**American-Style options**: Options that can be exercised at any time before the expiry of the contract. Eg stock and ETF options.

**European-Style options**: Options that can only be exercised at expiration. Eg most index options

**Opening transaction**: The action taken to enter an option contract

**Closing transaction**: The action taken to exit an option contract

**Premium**: The cost of the option

**Strike price**: The predetermined price at which the underlying asset can be bought or sold

**Expiration date**: The deadline in which you must decide to exercise your option by

**In the money**: A call option is ITM if the current price of the underlying stock is greater than the strike price. A put option is ITM if the current price is lower than the strike price

**Out of the money**: A call option is OTM if the current price is lower than the strike price. A put option is OTM if the current price is greater than the strike price. 

**At the money**: Call or put options are ATM if the strike price equals the price of the underlying asset
# Call vs Put options
**Call options**
Purchasing a call option enables you to purchase a specific security at a locked in price. You are buying the right to buy an asset at the strike price. If the price of the security rises you can make money by exercising the call contract and selling the stock at a price higher than the strike price. If the stock doesn't rise as expected, you can not exercise the option and only lose the premium paid.

**Put options**
Purchasing a put option gives you the right to sell a security at a locked in price sometime in the future. If the price falls, you can make money buy exercising the contract and selling the security at the strike price and purchasing it again at the lower market price. Put options are beneficial when you own a stock that you think may fall but you dont want to sell it yet.
# Factors affecting an options price 
**Underlying assets price**
- Most direct influence on option's price
- Call options become more valuable while put options decrease in value when a stocks price rises
- The opposite is true when a stock's price falls

**Time decay**
- Options decrease in value as they approach their expiration date
- Time decay accelerates as the expiration date approaches

**Market Volatility**
- Implied volatility is the likelihood that an assets price will change
- Higher volatility results in higher option prices regardless of if they are calls or puts. 

**Interest rates**
- Usually less significant than other factors
- Higher interest rates increase the cost of call options and decrease the cost of put options
- This is more noticeable in longer term options
