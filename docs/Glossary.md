### Hedging
A strategy to limit investment risk whereby two trades are made that are expected to move in opposite directions. This reduces potential risk as the trades cancel each other out but also reduces potential gains. 

### Arbitrage
Arbitrage is trading that exploits price differences between identical assets in multiple markets. The asset is purchased in one market and sold for a higher price in another and the difference in price is pocketed by the trader. Arbitrage takes advantage of market inefficiencies and allows the trader to make (mostly) risk free profit. 

### Risk Neutral Pricing
Risk neutral describes an investors appetite for risk. Risk neutral investors understand the risks involved but don't factor them into their decisions, while risk averse investors have a greater fear of losing money and avoid risky transactions. 

Risk-neutral probabilities price assets based on their potential future value without factoring in their risk, simplifying their pricing. This involves smoothing out extrema in the assets price to maintain a stable value, thereby minimising unusually high market outcomes while increasing the possible lows. These prices assume the absence of arbitrage.

### Volatility
Volatility is how much and how quickly prices move over a given span of time. For a stock, it is how much the price fluctuates around its mean price. Highly volatile assets are less predictable and thus considered riskier than less volatile assets. Volatility is often calculated using the variance or standard deviation (square root of variance) of returns. Let $V$ be the volatility, $\sigma$ be the standard deviation and $T$ be the number of periods in the time horizon. Then $V = \sigma\sqrt T$. Volatility can also be measured through beta coefficients or option pricing models. The VIX is a measure of the short term volatility in the broader market.
